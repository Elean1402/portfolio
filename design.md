# Design Brief

## Core Idea

The site should feel personal, loud, handmade, and game-like.

Think less "creative portfolio" and more:

* an old personal website from the 2000s
* a notebook someone was not expecting others to read
* a terminal session at 2:47 AM
* the menu screen of an indie game about isolation
* a mostly empty private room you were not exactly invited into

The site is a room, not a showroom.

It should feel like quietly snooping through a private space. The visitor should piece together who I am from what I left behind, rather than being guided through a polished portfolio.

## Room Direction

The main interface should be a mostly empty room or room-like scene.

A few things are visible because they matter:

* a desk
* an old monitor or computer
* a few desktop folders
* loose papers
* a notebook
* one shelf, corner, or small surface for project objects
* a poster, flyer, note, or window
* cables
* a save point object

Most of the room should stay empty. The emptiness is part of the mood.

Projects can appear as objects instead of cards: folders, paper stacks, little figures, broken devices, labels, boxes, or things sitting alone in a corner. Each project object should feel specific, slightly handmade, and emotionally connected to the work.

## Emotional Direction

The site should feel like a place someone built because they had to.

Not polished. Not chaotic. Tense.

The mood is quiet frustration, unfinished thoughts, late nights, and things left unsaid. It should feel closer to a game world, a zine, or a personal hideout than a portfolio.

The most important feeling is restrained intensity. Something is trying to break through the surface but never quite does.

## Visual Style

* No clean grids or corporate dashboard styling
* Personality over professionalism
* Atmosphere over efficiency
* Use negative space aggressively
* Prefer silence over decoration
* Loud in personality, sparse in layout
* Imperfections are allowed if they feel intentional
* Objects should feel placed by a person, not arranged by a template
* A few specific objects are stronger than many generic props

## Typography

* Headlines: `Instrument Serif`
* Labels and UI text: `Iosevka`
* Body copy: `Space Grotesk`

The contrast between a literary serif and a programmer's monospace should create tension rather than harmony.

## Structure

The site should have a small number of places, not traditional pages.

Home is the room. It is the emotional center of the site. Nothing should explain itself too quickly.

Number keys open four main sections:

1. About
2. Projects
3. Writing / Notes
4. Contact / Exit

`Escape` returns home.

Arrow keys change projects when the project view is active.

The site should reward looking around, but it should not punish visitors who just want to read.

## Interactions

Interactions should feel discovered, not presented.

Objects in the room can be inspected. Selecting an object should feel like opening a folder, picking up a note, or finding something hidden, not like clicking a portfolio card.

On mobile, keyboard interactions should become touch controls that still feel part of the world. Avoid standard app navigation if possible.

## Content and Voice

The writing should be informal, direct, personal, and slightly rough.

Developer experience should appear through the projects and writing, not through a highly technical interface.

Avoid marketing language, self-branding, and exaggerated confidence.

Write like a person, not a product.

## Do Not Overdo

The site can feel strange, but it should not become a gimmick.

Use 3D, sound, glitch, grain, hover states, and animation carefully. Effects should feel like pressure under the surface, not decoration placed on top.

Avoid overusing:

* full-screen grain
* scanlines
* glitch effects
* fake CRT styling
* loud sound effects
* constant animation
* excessive hover tricks
* too many hidden interactions
* ironic retro jokes
* fake operating system nostalgia
* clutter for realism
* props that do not reveal character or function
* labels that explain everything too clearly

The room should not become a cool bedroom simulator. It should feel selected, sparse, tense, and personal.

## Accessibility and Usability

The site can be unusual, but it still needs to be usable.

* Keyboard navigation should work clearly
* Focus states should be visible
* Text must remain readable
* Motion should be reduced when the user prefers reduced motion
* Important content should not depend only on sound, hover, or 3D movement
* Visitors should be able to reach About, Projects, Writing, and Contact without solving a puzzle

Discovery is good. Confusion is not the goal.
